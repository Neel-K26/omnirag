Hypertension: Clinical Overview

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is a chronic condition in which the force of blood
against artery walls is persistently elevated. It is typically diagnosed when systolic
blood pressure is 130 mmHg or higher, or diastolic pressure is 80 mmHg or higher, on
repeated measurements.

Hypertension is a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, including
stroke, myocardial infarction, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease. Most cases are
classified as primary (essential) hypertension, with no single identifiable cause, though
genetics, age, obesity, high sodium intake, physical inactivity, and chronic stress are
recognized contributors. Secondary hypertension, caused by an identifiable underlying
condition such as renal artery stenosis or endocrine disorders, accounts for a smaller
proportion of cases.

First-line management emphasizes lifestyle modification: reducing sodium intake, adopting
a diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy (such as the DASH diet), regular
aerobic exercise, weight management, limiting alcohol consumption, and smoking cessation.

When lifestyle changes are insufficient, pharmacological treatment is introduced.
Commonly used drug classes include:
- ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril) and ARBs (e.g., losartan), which relax blood vessels.
- Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine), which reduce vascular resistance.
- Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide), which reduce blood volume.
- Beta blockers, generally reserved for patients with specific comorbidities such as
  coronary artery disease.

Choice of agent depends on patient age, comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, kidney disease),
race-based response patterns documented in clinical guidelines, and tolerability of side
effects. Regular blood pressure monitoring and medication adherence are essential for
long-term risk reduction.
